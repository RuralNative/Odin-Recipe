Adobong Baboy - Sweet and Spicy Version

Adobong baboy (pork adobo) in sweet and spicy version is a popular variation of the classic Filipino adobo dish. This version is characterized by its sweet and tangy flavor, with a hint of spiciness that adds an extra kick to the dish.

Adobong baboy in sweet and spicy version is typically served over steamed rice and garnished with chopped green onions or cilantro. It can be enjoyed as a main course or as a side dish, and is perfect for sharing with family and friends.

Overall, adobong baboy in sweet and spicy version is a delicious and flavorful twist on the classic adobo dish, and is sure to satisfy anyone who loves a little sweetness and heat in their meals.

  • 1 kilogram of chicken (cut into serving pieces)
  • 1/2 cup of soy sauce
  • 1/2 cup of vinegar
  • 1 head of garlic (peeled and crushed)
  • 3-4 pieces of bay leaves
  • 1 teaspoon of whole peppercorns
  • 1 cup of water
  • 2 tablespoons of cooking oil

STEPS:

  1. In a large bowl, combine the soy sauce, vinegar, garlic, bay leaves, and whole peppercorns. Mix well.
  2. Add the chicken pieces to the marinade, making sure that each piece is well coated. Cover the bowl and refrigerate for at least 30 minutes, or preferably overnight.
  3. Heat the cooking oil in a large pan over medium-high heat. Once hot, add the chicken pieces (reserve the marinade) and fry until lightly browned on both sides. Remove the chicken from the pan and set aside.
  4. In the same pan, pour in the marinade and water. Bring the mixture to a boil, then reduce the heat to low and add the chicken back into the pan. Cover and let the chicken simmer for 30-40 minutes, or until the chicken is fully cooked and the sauce has thickened.
  5. Once the chicken is cooked, transfer it to a serving dish and pour the sauce over the top. Garnish with chopped green onions or cilantro, if desired.
  6. Serve hot with steamed rice and enjoy your delicious Pinoy-style adobong manok!

Note: You can adjust the amount of soy sauce and vinegar to your personal taste preference. Some people like their adobo to be more salty, while others prefer it to be more tangy.
